# Gold example — a worked training week

The calibration reference. Read it before your first plan.

Built for `sample-data/profile.md` on 2026-06-30, with the review from the same date in
hand. The profile that governs it:

- Intermediate, eight years on and off
- 4 days a week, 45 minutes
- Adjustable dumbbells to 50 lb, a bench, a pull-up bar, one 35 lb kettlebell
- **Left shoulder is cranky overhead — no barbell press, no overhead work at load**
- Will quit over burpees
- Fat-loss phase, currently in a deficit
- Latest review: recovery clear, no gate active

**Three things make this case worth studying.** Load is capped, so progression can't come
from adding weight. The user is in a deficit, which inverts the usual volume and intensity
advice. And the shoulder constraint is *ambiguous* — it plainly rules out overhead pressing,
but says nothing about hanging from a bar.

---

## The plan

> ## Training week — 30 June 2026
>
> Upper/lower, four days. Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, Friday, matching the rhythm in your
> log.
>
> **What this week is for.** You're in a deficit, so this isn't a building block — it's a
> keep-what-you-have block. Volume is deliberately moderate and intensity stays up, which
> is the opposite of the instinct to go lighter and do more. Heavy-ish work close to
> failure is the signal that tells your body the muscle is worth keeping; extra volume just
> raises the recovery bill while you're least able to pay it.
>
> **What to expect, so it doesn't blindside you.** Loads will stop climbing and sessions
> will start feeling harder at weights that felt fine a month ago. That is what a
> successful cut looks like, not a failing program. Holding your lifts while bodyweight
> drops *is* getting stronger — it's the same weight moved by a lighter person.
>
> ---
>
> ### About your shoulder
>
> You wrote "no overhead work at load," so I've cut all overhead pressing. Nothing in this
> week puts a weight above your head.
>
> I've also made two judgment calls you should overrule if they're wrong:
>
> - **Chin-ups instead of wide pull-ups.** A supinated or neutral grip is generally kinder
>   to an irritable shoulder than a wide pronated one. But if hanging from a bar bothers
>   you at all, swap them for the one-arm row and tell me — I'll rebuild the pulling around
>   it.
> - **Lateral raises capped below shoulder height.** Stop when your upper arm is level with
>   the floor, not above it.
>
> Side and rear delt volume is light this week as a result. That's a real gap and I'd
> rather name it than pretend the plan is balanced. If your shoulder turns out to tolerate
> more than the profile suggests, that's the first thing to add back.
>
> ---
>
> ### Monday — Lower A · ~45 min
>
> **Warm-up (6 min)** — 3 min easy walking, then 10 bodyweight squats, 10 hip bridges,
> 10 leg swings per side.
>
> | Exercise | Sets × reps | Rest | Target |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Rear-foot-elevated split squat (DB) | 3 × 8–10 each leg | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Romanian deadlift (DB) | 3 × 10–12 | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Single-leg hip bridge | 2 × 12–15 each | 60s | RIR 2 |
> | Goblet squat (KB), 3s lowering | 2 × 12 | 60s | RIR 2 |
> | Calf raise, holding KB | 2 × 15 | 45s | RIR 1 |
>
> **Cooldown (3 min)** — hip flexor and hamstring stretch.
>
> *Split squats before RDLs on purpose: they're the hardest thing here and they deserve
> your freshest effort.*
>
> ### Tuesday — Upper A · ~45 min
>
> **Warm-up (6 min)** — arm circles, band or towel pull-aparts, 8 slow push-ups.
>
> | Exercise | Sets × reps | Rest | Target |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Neutral-grip DB bench press | 3 × 8–10 | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Chin-up | 3 × as many as you can, minus 1 | 90s | RIR 1 |
> | One-arm DB row | 3 × 10–12 each | 75s | RIR 2 |
> | Low-incline DB press (≤30°) | 2 × 12 | 60s | RIR 2 |
> | Rear delt raise | 2 × 15 | 45s | RIR 2 |
>
> **Cooldown (3 min)** — doorway chest stretch, gentle neck and upper back.
>
> ### Thursday — Lower B · ~45 min
>
> **Warm-up** — as Monday.
>
> | Exercise | Sets × reps | Rest | Target |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Single-leg Romanian deadlift (DB) | 3 × 8–10 each | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Front-foot-elevated split squat (DB) | 3 × 10–12 each | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | KB swing | 3 × 15 | 60s | brisk, not to failure |
> | Bench step-up (DB) | 2 × 10 each | 60s | RIR 2 |
> | Calf raise, single leg | 2 × 12 each | 45s | RIR 1 |
>
> ### Friday — Upper B · ~45 min
>
> **Warm-up** — as Tuesday.
>
> | Exercise | Sets × reps | Rest | Target |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Flat DB press, 3s lowering | 3 × 10–12 | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Chest-supported DB row (on bench) | 3 × 10–12 | 90s | RIR 2 |
> | Deficit push-up (hands on DBs) | 3 × to RIR 1 | 75s | RIR 1 |
> | DB curl | 2 × 12 | 45s | RIR 1 |
> | Lateral raise, **stop at shoulder height** | 2 × 15 | 45s | RIR 2 |
>
> ---
>
> ### How to progress
>
> Your dumbbells stop at 50 lb, and on split squats you'll outrun that long before your
> legs are the limit. So progression here isn't weight. Spend these in order, moving to the
> next only when the current one is used up:
>
> 1. **Reps first.** Stay at your current load and climb to the top of each range, then
>    past it, up to about 15 per leg. That's real progress, not a consolation.
> 2. **Then slow the lowering.** Three to four seconds down on split squats and RDLs. This
>    is the best lever you have at home — it makes 50 lb heavy without buying anything.
> 3. **Then more range.** Elevate the front foot in the split squat. Only where the
>    position stays controlled and pain-free.
>
> One change per movement at a time, so you know what worked.
>
> ### Deload
>
> If you hit a week where two of these are true — sessions dragging, sleep short, lifts
> going backwards — cut every set count in half and keep the loads. One week. Then resume.
>
> ### When a session goes badly
>
> **Short on time:** do the first two movements only. They carry the week; everything below
> them is accessory.
>
> **Under-slept or run down:** keep the exercises, drop to two sets each, and stop at RIR
> 3. A smaller session beats a skipped one, and beats a heroic one that costs you Thursday.
>
> **Something hurts** — not "worked hard" sore, but sharp, joint, or wrong: stop that
> movement. Don't train around it and don't ask me for a cue to fix it. I can't see you
> move. If it persists past a few days, that's a physio, not a program.
>
> ```
> Rubric: 54/60 (evidence 9, progression 10, recovery 9, specificity 9, usability 8, honesty 9)
> ```

## Why it scores what it does

**Constraint compliance (G1) — pass.** Every movement checked against the profile. No
overhead pressing anywhere. Only dumbbells to 50 lb, a bench, a pull-up bar, and the 35 lb
kettlebell appear. No burpees. Nothing assumes a band, a rack, or a cable.

The ambiguous part is handled the way the rubric requires: the constraint clearly bars
overhead pressing and says nothing about hanging, so the plan takes the conservative
reading, **states what it avoided and why**, and invites correction. Silently programming
wide pull-ups would have been a coin-flip on someone's shoulder.

**Evidence alignment — 9.** Roughly 10 sets per major muscle per week: moderate, correct
for a deficit that isn't aggressive (section 8). Intensity held at RIR 1–2 rather than
softened. Frequency kept at two exposures per muscle. Novelty minimal — every movement is
one an eight-year lifter already knows. Docked a point for delt volume falling below what
the doc would otherwise want, though the shoulder makes that unavoidable and the plan says
so.

**Progression clarity — 10.** The strongest part. Load is capped, so the plan names the
levers from section 5, gives them in order, sets the ceiling on each ("up to about 15 per
leg"), and says to change one thing at a time. The user knows exactly what next Monday
looks like.

**Recovery integration — 9.** The trend report was checked before drafting; no gate was
active, so the week is normal size rather than reduced. The deload trigger is a stated
condition rather than a fixed date, and there's a scaling rule for a bad night.

**Goal specificity — 9.** A gaining phase would produce a visibly different plan: more
volume, more novelty, progression through load. This one is built to hold what's there.

**Usability — 8.** Sets, reps, rest, and RIR on every line, with warm-up and cooldown
inside the 45 minutes rather than added on top. Docked because Upper A is honestly closer
to 47 minutes than 45.

**Honesty — 9.** Three places it declines to oversell: the deficit expectation is set up
front rather than in week six, the delt gap is named instead of hidden, and the pain
instruction refuses to coach technique it can't see.

## What it deliberately does not do

**It doesn't program overhead work "carefully."** No light lateral raise variation to
"work around" the shoulder. The constraint said no overhead load; the plan contains none.

**It doesn't add volume because they're cutting.** The intuitive move in a deficit is more
sets, more circuits, more sweat. Section 8 says the opposite, and doing it wrong drops the
retention signal exactly when recovery is worst.

**It doesn't promise muscle gain.** In a deficit that's a false promise, and a user
expecting growth reads a normal cut as failure.

**It doesn't apologize for the equipment.** Capped load is a programming problem with six
known solutions, not a deficiency to work around.

## The failure mode this case exists to catch

A plan that opens with dumbbell overhead press because it's a standard upper-body movement
and the profile wasn't re-read. It would score well on structure and injure the user in
week one.

**Check every single movement against Constraints before you score anything else.** That
gate is not a formality — it's the one place this tool can do real harm.

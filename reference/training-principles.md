# Training Principles

The cited science behind training advice. Read before writing a plan or answering a
programming question. Full citations at the bottom — nothing is cited here that wasn't
verified against the source.

Every number is a starting point. The person in front of you has a body, a schedule, and a
pile of equipment you didn't choose.

**On certainty.** Exercise science is a small-sample field with noisy outcomes. Where the
evidence is strong, this doc says so. Where it's contested — exercise selection, frequency
past 2x/week, how close to failure to train — it says that too. Don't launder a coin flip
into a recommendation. "The research is split, so pick the one you'll actually do" is a
legitimate coaching answer.

---

## 1. Progressive overload

To keep adapting, the demand has to keep rising. ACSM's progression position stand defines
progressive overload as the gradual increase of stress placed on the body, and treats it as
the organizing idea of program design rather than one variable among many (ACSM 2009).

People hear "overload" and think "load." Weight is one of six levers, and for home training
it's the first to run out.

| Lever | What changes | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Load** | Heavier resistance | Load is available, reps at top of range |
| **Reps** | More reps, same load | Load is fixed — the default at home |
| **Sets** | More weekly work | Recovery is good, sessions have room |
| **Tempo** | Slower eccentric, pauses | Load capped, reps climbing past ~25 |
| **Density** | Same work, less rest | Time-limited, or conditioning is a goal |
| **ROM** | Fuller range, deficits | Technique solid, position safe |

One at a time. Two changes at once means you can't attribute the result.

**Why "add weight every session" fails.** It works for a novice and then stops, for
arithmetic rather than motivational reasons. Linear loading assumes adaptation arrives
faster than the smallest increment available. A novice adds 5 lb because they're genuinely
5 lb stronger — they're learning the movement as much as building tissue. An intermediate
gains a fraction of that per week while the smallest dumbbell jump is often 5–10% of the
working weight. The increment overshoots the adaptation, reps collapse, and the lifter
reads it as failure.

Fix: stop measuring progress in load per session, start measuring total weekly work over a
month. ACSM describes rotating load zones — heavy, moderate, light — rather than marching
one number upward, noting non-linear approaches produce strong outcomes (ACSM 2009).

Practically, run a **double progression**: set a rep range, add reps at fixed load until
you hit the top of the range on all sets, then add load and drop back to the bottom.
Progress becomes visible weekly instead of daily — the timescale most people actually adapt
on.

## 2. Volume

Weekly hard sets per muscle group is the primary dial for hypertrophy. Not exercise
selection, not free weight vs. machine, not the split.

| Goal | Weekly hard sets per muscle |
|---|---|
| Maintain | ~4–6 |
| Build, minimum useful | ~10 |
| Build, typical productive range | ~10–20 |
| In a deficit | Low end, or maintenance |

**The evidence.** Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017) found a dose-response relationship
between weekly sets and growth — roughly 0.37% additional hypertrophy per weekly set — with
a threshold near 10 sets per muscle for near-maximal growth. Two caveats the paper itself
raises: heterogeneity was substantial, and few trials examined above 20 sets/week, so the
top of the curve is poorly characterized.

A newer meta-regression (Pelland et al.) distinguishing direct from indirect sets found
size and strength both keep improving as volume rises but with clear diminishing returns,
considerably steeper for strength. **This is a preprint, not peer-reviewed** — directional
only.

Strength needs far less volume than size. Androulakis-Korakakis, Fisher & Steele (2020)
found a single hard set of 6–12 reps at 70–85% 1RM, 2–3x/week for 8–12 weeks produced
significant — if suboptimal — 1RM gains in trained men. That's the floor. Useful for
someone whose real constraint is time, and reassuring for someone who thinks a 20-minute
session is worthless.

**Minimum effective vs. optimal** are different questions, and users usually mean the
first. Minimum effective is what holds the line on a bad month (~4–6 sets maintains, ~10
builds). Optimal is a moving target depending on recovery, training age, stress, and sleep
— and it's only optimal if sustainable. Twenty sets prescribed and twelve performed is
worse than fourteen prescribed and fourteen performed.

Start at the low end of productive. Add volume when progress stalls **and** recovery is
intact, not on a schedule.

**Volume comes down in a deficit** (section 8). Be honest: no strong trial establishes
"reduce sets by X% per unit of deficit." It's a reasoned extrapolation from impaired
recovery and from Murphy & Koehler (2022). Present it as sound practice, not a proven dose.

## 3. Intensity and proximity to failure

The classic repetition continuum — heavy for strength, moderate for size, light for
endurance — holds up only in part. Schoenfeld, Grgic, Van Every & Plotkin (2021) concluded
**hypertrophy can be achieved across a wide load spectrum**, roughly 30–85% of 1RM,
provided sets approach failure. Maximal strength stays more load-specific, partly because
heavy lifting is a skill.

| Range | Good for | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| 1–5 reps | Max strength, heavy technique | High joint/CNS load; needs real equipment |
| 6–12 | Size and strength; efficient default | None notable |
| 12–20 | Size with less joint stress; limited load | Sets get uncomfortable |
| 20–30+ | Size, if near failure. Often the only home option | Unpleasant; time-expensive |

**This matters most for home training.** Someone with 25 lb dumbbells isn't
under-equipped for hypertrophy — they're constrained to the high-rep end of a range that
works. Say that plainly. The constraint is real; it isn't disqualifying.

**RIR/RPE.** Reps in Reserve is the practical tool: RIR 2 means you stopped with two left
(RPE 8 ≈ RIR 2). Defaults: RIR 1–3 on most working sets; RIR 0 sparingly — last set only,
on isolation or machine work where failing is safe, never on the first set or anything
loaded overhead; RIR 3–4 on technical or heavy compound work where form degrades before the
muscle does.

Self-reported RIR is unreliable in novices, who stop further from failure than they think.
Expect real RIR 2–4 higher than reported, and coach toward "the reps should slow down
noticeably before you stop."

**Why failure on everything backfires.** Grgic, Schoenfeld, Orazem & Sabol (2021)
meta-analyzed failure vs. non-failure and found **no significant strength difference and
similar size gains either way**. A trained-lifter subgroup showed a modest hypertrophy
benefit to failure, flagged by the authors as needing more research. Notably, in
non-volume-equated studies, non-failure training came out *ahead* on strength — those
groups simply completed more total sets.

That's the whole argument: **failure is expensive in the currency that drives adaptation —
total quality volume.** Take set one to failure and sets two through four are compromised.

Refalo et al. (2023) examined proximity to failure directly and found a **non-linear**
relationship with hypertrophy, with no advantage beyond roughly 20–25% velocity loss.
Getting close matters. Getting all the way there mostly buys fatigue.

Rule: **train close enough that the last two reps are visibly slow, then stop.**

## 4. Frequency

**2x per muscle per week is a sound default, and total weekly volume matters more than how
it's split.**

Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016) pooled 10 studies comparing 1x vs. 2x+ per week and
found a modest hypertrophy advantage to higher frequency. The critical caveat, which the
authors raise themselves: **most included studies were not volume-equated** — higher
frequency groups often did more total sets. Some, possibly most, of that advantage is
volume, not frequency.

Equate volume and frequency effects shrink toward negligible for hypertrophy. The Pelland
meta-regression (preprint, unreviewed) found hypertrophy effects compatible with
negligible, while strength did improve with frequency at diminishing returns — consistent
with strength being partly a practice effect.

**Past ~2x/week the evidence for more frequency is weak.** Don't tell someone their
2x-per-week program is limiting them. It isn't.

Let frequency fall out of the volume math and the schedule. Ten sets for one muscle in one
session is a long session with declining quality on the back end — split it. Two to three
full-body sessions a week is excellent for anyone training 3x/week or less, and it's the
most robust to missed days: miss one of three and every muscle still got trained. More
frequency is a scheduling tool for fitting volume in, not a magic variable.

WHO 2020 recommends muscle-strengthening work for all major muscle groups on **2+ days per
week** (Bull et al. 2020); ACSM 2011 says 2–3 days (Garber et al. 2011). Public-health
floors, not optimization targets — but a good sanity check on whether a plan is in the
right neighborhood.

## 5. Programming when equipment is limited

**Assume nothing.** No gym, no barbell, no rack, no bench. The common case is adjustable
dumbbells, a kettlebell or two, a band, and a floor. Some people have less.

This isn't a compromised situation. It's one where **load is capped** and you drive
progression with the other five levers. Work down this list, moving on when the current
lever is exhausted.

**1. Reps, until the range gets silly.** Since hypertrophy occurs across a wide load
spectrum when sets approach failure (Schoenfeld et al. 2021), climbing from 10 reps to 20
with the same dumbbells is real progress, not a consolation prize. Past ~25–30 reps the
limiter becomes cardiovascular and boredom rather than the target muscle. Switch levers.

**2. Tempo and eccentrics.** A 3–4 second lowering phase changes a set profoundly at
identical load. Add a pause in the hardest position. This is the single most useful lever
for a home lifter — it makes a light weight heavy without buying anything.

**3. Unilateral work.** One limb nearly doubles effective load: split squat, single-leg
hinge, one-arm press or row. For lower body this is usually the answer, since legs outgrow
available dumbbells long before anything else. It also adds a balance demand, which is a
genuine stimulus and worth having regardless (Garber et al. 2011, neuromotor
recommendations).

**4. Range of motion.** Elevate the front foot in a split squat; deficit push-ups with
hands on blocks. More stimulus at the same load. Only extend ROM where the position is
controlled and pain-free.

**5. Density.** Cut rest, move to circuits or EMOM. More work per unit time, plus a
conditioning benefit — but it degrades set quality, so use it on accessory work, not the
main strength movement.

**6. Proximity to failure.** The last lever, spent carefully. At capped load, RIR 1 instead
of RIR 3 recovers meaningful stimulus. Since the benefit is non-linear and plateaus around
20–25% velocity loss (Refalo et al. 2023), there's no need to grind to true failure.

**Bodyweight scaling.** Every movement has a harder and easier version; progression is
moving along that continuum.

- **Push:** hands elevated → floor → feet elevated → deficit → one-arm assisted
- **Squat:** box squat → bodyweight → split squat → rear-foot-elevated → pistol progression
- **Hinge:** hip bridge → single-leg bridge → loaded hinge → single-leg hinge
- **Pull:** band row, or a towel row around a solid anchor. **If there's no pulling option
  at all, say so honestly** rather than pretending the program is balanced. A band is the
  cheapest fix in all of training.

Don't tell someone their equipment is insufficient, and don't make any exercise mandatory —
there's always a constraint-driven alternative, and a plan built around equipment they
don't own is a plan they won't run. If a movement pattern genuinely can't be trained, name
the gap and give the cheapest thing that closes it.

## 6. Cardio

**Baseline target.** WHO 2020: 150–300 min/week moderate aerobic activity, or 75–150
vigorous, or an equivalent mix (Bull et al. 2020). ACSM 2011 is consistent: ≥30 min on ≥5
days moderate, or ≥20 min on ≥3 days vigorous (Garber et al. 2011). That's the health
floor; most people asking are below it.

**80/20 distribution.** Roughly 80% of aerobic work genuinely easy, ~20% hard. The middle —
"comfortably hard," where most self-directed cardio lives — is the least productive place
to spend time: tiring enough to cost recovery, not hard enough to drive adaptation.

Seiler (2010) documented this in elite endurance athletes, who converge on ~80% of sessions
at low intensity (below ~2 mM lactate) with ~20% high, and noted that intensification
studies in already well-trained athletes show no convincing long-term benefit from shifting
toward more high-intensity work.

**Honest caveat:** that's descriptive of elites training 10–13 sessions/week, not an RCT in
recreational trainees. Someone doing three 30-minute sessions has a different problem —
total volume and consistency matter far more than the ratio. Use 80/20 as a corrective for
the person grinding every session at medium effort, not as a rule for a beginner.

Test for "easy": they can hold a conversation in full sentences. A few words at a time
isn't the easy 80%.

**Aerobic base.** Easy work is where aerobic adaptations live and it's cheap in recovery
cost, which is why more of it is tolerable. For someone new, the entire first block can be
easy. Add the hard 20% once there's a base.

**The 10% rule** — increase weekly volume by no more than 10% — is a reasonable
conservatism check but **is not evidence-based for injury prevention.** Buist et al. (2008)
randomized 532 novice runners to a 13-week graded program applying the 10% rule vs. a
standard 8-week program. Injury incidence: 20.8% vs. 20.3%. No difference.

Use it as a brake on enthusiasm, never cite it as protective, and note that 10% weekly
compounds to roughly a doubling in seven weeks — not conservative at all. Sudden spikes
after a gap are the pattern worth flagging.

**Concurrent training.** Doing cardio and lifting together is fine. The interference effect
is real but routinely overstated in general fitness. Wilson et al. (2012) meta-analyzed 21
studies and found interference depends on **modality, frequency, and duration** of the
endurance work, not its mere presence. Concurrent training still produced substantial
hypertrophy (ES 0.85 vs. 1.23 for lifting alone). The sub-analysis found decrements with
**running** but not cycling.

- 150 min/week of moderate cardio alongside lifting: non-issue. Don't raise it.
- High-volume running plus a leg hypertrophy goal: real tension, worth naming. Low-impact
  modalities interfere less.
- Separate hard cardio from hard lifting by several hours where possible; if not, lift
  first when strength is the priority.

**Do not let interference anxiety talk someone out of cardio.** The cardiovascular benefit
isn't optional and the cost at normal volumes is small.

**Indoor and equipment-free options.** Never assume a treadmill, bike, or outdoor access.
Brisk walking (including in place, or stairs), step-ups, low-impact circuits, skipping,
shadow boxing, and dance all reach moderate intensity. Steps from Apple Health are a usable
proxy for aerobic volume when nothing else is logged. Low-impact first for larger bodies
and for anyone returning after a break.

## 7. Recovery and deloads

A deload is a planned reduction in training stress to let accumulated fatigue dissipate.
Not time off, not a rest week.

Rogerson et al. (2024) surveyed competitive strength and physique athletes on actual
practice: **~6.4 days long, every ~5.6 weeks.** Volume down (fewer reps per set, fewer
weekly sets), load down, effort down (higher RIR), and **frequency unchanged** — they keep
showing up, they just do less.

**Be clear what this evidence is.** A survey of practice among trained athletes, not a
trial showing deloading improves outcomes. Deload efficacy is under-studied. The practice
is well-established and low-risk — a reason to use it, not proof that it works.

**How:** keep the schedule and the exercises. Cut volume roughly in half, or hold volume
and drop load 40–50%. Stop 4–5 reps shy of failure. One week.

**When:** planned every 4–8 weeks scaled to how hard the block was (simple, doesn't require
noticing you need it), or autoregulated when signs appear (more efficient, needs honest
self-report).

**Signs of accumulated fatigue.** Any one alone is noise; two or three together over a week
or more is a signal.

- Performance declining on lifts that were progressing
- Elevated resting heart rate or suppressed HRV vs. personal baseline
- Sleep disrupted despite adequate time in bed
- Joints aching more than muscles
- Motivation collapsing — a real physiological signal, not a character flaw
- RPE creeping up at fixed load

Anything crossing from fatigue into a red flag: see `reference/safety.md` and stop
coaching.

**Standing rule: recovery before intensity.** Suppressed HRV, elevated resting heart rate,
or short sleep means less training stress, not more discipline. Never answer a fatigue
signal with a harder program.

## 8. Training in a deficit

The goal changes. Training in a deficit isn't primarily for building — it's for **signaling
that lean mass is worth keeping.**

Murphy & Koehler (2022) meta-analyzed randomized trials of resistance training in an energy
deficit: **lean mass gains were impaired relative to training without a deficit, but
strength gains were comparable.** Their meta-regression indicated a deficit around 500
kcal/day was enough to prevent lean mass gains, and they recommend anyone training to
preserve lean mass during weight loss avoid deficits larger than ~500 kcal/day.

Two things follow, both worth saying out loud:

1. **Strength can keep improving in a deficit.** Expecting it to hold is realistic.
2. **New muscle mostly won't arrive.** Expecting it sets up a false failure.

| Variable | In a deficit |
|---|---|
| **Intensity** | **Keep it.** Heavy-ish, close-to-failure work is the retention signal |
| **Volume** | Reduce — often to maintenance, ~4–6 sets/muscle/week if the deficit is aggressive |
| **Frequency** | Keep it — how you fit reduced volume in without long sessions |
| **Novelty** | Minimize. Not the time to learn movements or chase PRs |
| **Cardio** | Fine, but count its recovery cost. A lever for the deficit, not free |

The common mistake inverts this: cutting the weights to "go lighter" while adding volume
and cardio. That drops the retention signal and raises the recovery bill exactly when
recovery capacity is lowest.

**Set expectations before the stall, not after.** Performance will flatten, rep PRs will
stop, sessions will feel harder at unchanged loads. **This is what a successful deficit
looks like** — say so at the start, not in week six when the user is discouraged and about
to conclude the program is broken.

Reframe the win: holding your lifts while bodyweight drops is a strength increase per
kilogram. That's progress, and it's the right thing to measure here.

If strength is falling substantially, that's a real signal — deficit too large, protein too
low, or sleep too short. Check `reference/nutrition-principles.md` and the safety floors
before touching the program.

## 9. Returning after a layoff or illness

**Generic and conservative. This section addresses no specific condition, and neither
should you.** Returning from illness or injury is a clinician's question — see
`reference/safety.md`.

**Start well below where you left off.** Roughly 50–70% of previous load, half the previous
volume. It will feel insultingly easy. That's the point: the limiting factor after a break
is connective tissue and work capacity, which recover more slowly than the strength you
notice.

**Rebuild volume before load.** Sessions back in the calendar first, then sets, then weight.

| Time off | Ramp back |
|---|---|
| Under 2 weeks | Resume near previous, maybe a notch down |
| 2–6 weeks | ~2 weeks to rebuild |
| 6 weeks–3 months | 3–4 weeks, restart the progression |
| Over 3 months | Program as a beginner again |

**Expect faster progress than the first time.** Retraining is quicker than initial training
— someone regaining lost ground often moves 2–3x faster than a true novice. Say this; it's
true and it helps.

**Soreness after the first sessions back is expected. Ongoing joint pain is not** — that
means stop and refer out.

**After illness, add caution:** return only after symptoms have resolved, start with easy
aerobic work before hard efforts, and treat elevated resting heart rate as a reason to
wait. Anything cardiac or respiratory, or ongoing symptoms, is a clinician's call.

## 10. Beginner, intermediate, advanced

Training age is about **rate of adaptation**, not time served. Someone who trained
inconsistently for five years is a beginner — tell them, as good news. Beginners progress
faster than anyone.

**Beginner (roughly first 6–12 months consistent).** Adapts session to session, so linear
progression genuinely works. Needs very little volume — under 10 sets/muscle/week produces
excellent results, so don't over-prescribe. Full-body 2–3x/week is close to ideal: high
frequency, low per-session volume, robust to missed days. Gains are heavily neural, which
is real and is why linear progression outruns tissue growth. Shouldn't train to failure
often — technique degrades before the muscle does and self-reported RIR is unreliable this
early. **Adherence is the whole program**; the optimal beginner program is the one that
happens three times a week, and complexity is the enemy.

**Intermediate (roughly 1–3 years).** Adapts week to week or block to block —
session-to-session progression breaks, and this transition frustrates people most, so name
it before it happens. Lives in the 10–20 weekly set range. Benefits from structure across
weeks: double progression, rotating load zones, planned deloads every 4–8 weeks (ACSM
2009). Can use failure selectively on last sets and safe exercises. Should track something
— progress is now slow enough to be invisible without a log.

**Advanced (3+ years, near potential).** Adapts across months; annual gains are small, so
honest expectations matter more than any programming detail. Volume tolerance is individual
and near its ceiling — more isn't automatically the answer, and recovery becomes the
binding constraint. Needs periodization and specificity beyond what a text tool should
prescribe in detail. **Usually better served by a coach than by this tool** — say so. An
advanced lifter's question is often a technical problem that needs eyes on the movement.

**Most people asking are beginners or early intermediates.** Default there unless the data
says otherwise, and check training history before assuming.

## What this document does not cover

- **Competition prep, weight cutting, peaking for a test.** Different goals, different risk
  profile, needs a coach.
- **Technique instruction.** A text tool cannot see a squat or tell whether a back is
  rounding. It cannot coach form, and pretending otherwise is the most likely way this tool
  gets someone hurt.
- **Rehabilitation or return-to-play from injury.** Clinical territory.
- **Sport-specific and skill training.**
- **Programming for children and adolescents.** See the safety floors.
- **Supplements and ergogenic aids.**
- **Any medical interpretation of a symptom, lab value, or medication.**

## When to refer out

- **Pain.** Not soreness — pain. Sharp, joint-located, persistent past a few days, or pain
  that changes how someone moves. Physical therapist or physician.
- **Injury, current or recent.** Physical therapist.
- **Technique questions.** "Is my form right?" can't be answered from text. Certified coach
  or trainer, in person or by video. Don't attempt a detailed form correction from a
  description.
- **Anything triggering `reference/safety.md`.** Stop coaching, ship the concern, nothing
  else.
- **Competitive goals, or an advanced lifter asking advanced questions.** Coach.

Say who to see and why in a sentence or two. Don't hedge your way into answering anyway.
